Sunday — Opening Day

First day on site · ease in, soak it up
Morning Option
Wayfaring Walk — Guided walk through the stunning Bannau Brycheiniog National Park surrounds. A gentle way to arrive into the festival rhythm before the tents fill up. Walks depart from site, typically ~90 mins.
11:30
Reverend Richard Coles Booked Sold Out
A Heist Before Bedtime — funny true stories for all the family
Who: Former pop star (The Communards), Church of England priest, beloved BBC Radio 4 presenter of Saturday Live for 11 years, and best-selling crime novelist. One of Britain's most unlikely and endearing public figures.

What: True stories from his first children's book — the theft of Einstein's brain, the great maple syrup heist, and McDonald's involvement in a million-dollar crime. Uproarious storytelling that works for all ages. Perfect opener.
Meadow Stage · £10
16:00
Public Philosophy AI Pick
Ideas for everyday life
What: Hay always runs philosophy sessions that make big ideas accessible and debatable — the kind of thing you end up talking about all evening. Check the specific speaker closer to the day.

Why: Great palate cleanser between morning and headliners. Gets the group thinking and disagreeing — ideal warm-up for supper conversation.
17:30
Empire of AI AI Pick
The power, politics, and future of artificial intelligence
What: AI is one of Hay 2026's headline themes. Tim Berners-Lee is at the festival discussing This is for Everyone: Reclaiming the Web in the Age of AI. This session tackles how AI is reshaping power structures, who controls it, and what it means for all of us.

Why: Right in Adam's professional wheelhouse. Big questions about agency, control, and whether we're building something liberating or something that owns us.
🎲 Wildcards for Sunday
10:00 · David Miliband, Susan Neiman & Philippe Sands — Where Does Europe Fit in the New World Order?
Former Foreign Secretary, a philosopher, and an international law expert tackle Europe's relevance. Sharp, heavyweight, timely. Discovery Stage, £20.
10:00 · Claudia Hammond — Overwhelmed: Ways to Take the Pressure Off
BBC Radio 4's All in the Mind presenter. Evidence-based strategies for imposter syndrome, procrastination, and modern overload. Meadow Stage, £16.
11:30 · America 250: Revolution — Churchwell, Olusoga & Pearsall
250 years after the Declaration of Independence — what's really changed? Myths, empire, and revolution re-examined. Discovery Stage, £18.
13:00 · Michael Morpurgo with Jenny Agutter & the Brodsky Quartet
Poetry and narration through a live performance of Vivaldi's Four Seasons. A unique, beautiful one-off. Global Stage, £24.
Between sessions — lose yourself in Hay's legendary bookshops

Monday — Bank Holiday

Festival in full flow · the site will be buzzing
11:30
Tim Spector — Fermentation Booked
The gut health guru on the science of fermented foods
Who: Professor of Genetic Epidemiology at King's College London. Co-founder of ZOE. Author of Food for Life and Spoon-Fed — massive bestsellers that changed how millions think about food.

What: A deep dive into fermentation — why it matters, what the science says, how to do it properly. Spector makes complex science feel urgent and personal. Expect to leave wanting to make your own kimchi.
19:00
Hans Ulrich Obrist Pilar Pick
The world's most influential curator
Who: Artistic Director of the Serpentine Galleries. Widely considered the most powerful curator in contemporary art. Over 3,000 hours of artist interviews. ArtReview's most influential person in the art world, repeatedly.

What: Pilar's recommendation — and for good reason. A rare combination of creative ambition and philosophical curiosity. Whether discussing AI and art, the future of institutions, or his conversations with Bowie and Koolhaas, this will be unlike anything else at the festival.
🎲 Wildcards for Monday
TBC · Sophie Raworth interviews Kate Raworth — Doughnut Economics
BBC presenter interviews her economist sister, whose Doughnut model is being adopted by cities worldwide as a blueprint for sustainable growth.
TBC · Mariana Mazzucato — The Common Good Economy
The economist who coined "the entrepreneurial state." Advised governments worldwide on innovation policy. Provocative and brilliant.
TBC · Devi Sridhar & Dan Davis — How Not to Die (Too Soon)
Public health expert busts myths about longevity. Pairs perfectly with the Spector session earlier.

Tuesday — Crime Day Spotlight

Espionage & AI collide · last day together as four
11:30
Ben Macintyre — Spies, Lies and Skulduggery Pilar Pick
The secretive history of espionage
Who: One of Britain's greatest narrative non-fiction writers. Associate Editor at The Times. His spy books — A Spy Among Friends (Apple TV+), The Spy and the Traitor, Operation Mincemeat (Netflix) — read like thrillers but are meticulously researched true stories.

What: Double agents, betrayals, absurd bravery. His storytelling is cinematic and gripping. Pilar knows what she's doing with this one.
17:30
Jamie Bartlett — How to Talk to AI AI Pick
A practical guide to the AI conversation
Who: Author of The Dark Net and The People Vs Tech. Presenter of The Missing Cryptoqueen, one of the most downloaded BBC podcasts in history.

What: How do we actually interact with AI? What should we ask? What should we be wary of? Bartlett explains technology in human terms. Perfect for Adam's interests — useful for everyone.
🎲 Wildcards for Tuesday
TBC · Val McDermid — Crime Day Headliner
Queen of Scottish crime fiction, 40+ books. Headlines Crime Day with Fflur Dafydd for Silent Bones. Unmissable for thriller fans.
TBC · Antony Beevor — Rasputin and the Downfall of the Romanovs
Britain's foremost military historian gives us Rasputin. The man who wrote Stalingrad turns to history's most extraordinary character.
TBC · Irvine Welsh & Kevin Rowland — Bless Me Father
Trainspotting author with the Dexys frontman on Rowland's memoir. Two iconoclasts, one stage. Expect fireworks.

Thursday — The Big Day

Three sessions back to back · bring snacks
10:00
US Politics Session AI Pick
America under the microscope
What: 2026 marks 250 years since the Declaration of Independence. Hay has a strong US politics thread — sharp analysis of the post-election landscape, culture wars, global implications, and what comes next.

Why: The US shapes everything — markets, technology, geopolitics. Worth understanding from people who've spent decades studying it.
13:00
Bear Grylls — My Life in Books Booked
with podcaster Jamie Laing
Who: The world's most famous adventurer. Former SAS reservist, youngest Brit to summit Everest at 23, Chief Scout. A genuinely extraordinary life — from a freefall parachuting accident that broke his back to crossing the North Atlantic in an inflatable boat.

What: Part of Hay's "My Life in Books" series. Bear shares the books that shaped him. Expect to be surprised by what an action man reads — and inspired by how stories fuelled his adventures.
17:30
Technology, Women & Africa AI Pick
How tech is reshaping a continent
What: How technology is transforming Africa — particularly through the lens of women leading that change. Innovation, mobile banking revolutions, and the leapfrogging of Western infrastructure.

Why: A refreshing counterpoint to Western-centric AI conversations. Real stories of real impact.
🎲 Wildcards for Thursday
TBC · Jeremy Bowen — Christopher Hitchens Lecture: War, Peace & the Pursuit of Truth
The BBC's most experienced war correspondent delivers Hay's most prestigious lecture. 30+ years in every major conflict zone. Unmissable.
TBC · Serhii Plokhy — The Harvard Lecture on Ukraine
Harvard's professor of Ukrainian history. Essential context for the war and its global ripple effects.
TBC · Michail Antonio & James Bloodworth — Modern Masculinity
Premier League footballer and investigative journalist tackle masculinity head-on. Honest, urgent, probably uncomfortable in the best way.

Friday — Nature & Power

Outdoors meets big-stage conversations
Morning Option
Guided Nature Walk — Led by guides from the Bannau Brycheiniog National Park. Explore the landscape around Hay-on-Wye. A chance to clear your head and remember that not everything worth thinking about happens in a tent.

AC Grayling AI Pick
Philosophy for the modern world
Who: One of Britain's foremost public philosophers. Founder and Master of the New College of the Humanities. Author of over 30 books. Fellow of the Royal Society of Literature.

What: The intersection of philosophy, politics, and our changing world. Vocal on Brexit, AI ethics, and the crisis of liberal democracy. Sharp, eloquent, unafraid to take a position.

Nicola Sturgeon AI Pick
Former First Minister of Scotland
Who: Scotland's longest-serving First Minister (2014–2023), first woman in the role. Led Scotland through the pandemic, Brexit aftermath, and the independence debate.

What: Taking stock of the changing landscape of British politics. Reflections on leadership, resilience, and what post-election Britain looks like. A strong conversationalist who doesn't duck difficult questions.

Saturday — Grand Finale

Last full day · go out on a high
10:00
Peace & Conflict Session AI Pick
Understanding a world at war
What: Hay 2026 has a strong peace and conflict strand — Palestinian writer Aziz Abu Sarah and Israeli writer Maoz Inon discuss The Future is Peace with BBC Chief International Correspondent Lyse Doucet.

Why: In a world that feels increasingly fractured, these are the conversations that matter most. Real people with real stakes. Start the last day with something that counts.
13:00
Kate & Sophie Raworth — Doughnut Economics Booked
BBC News presenter interviews her economist sister
Who: Kate Raworth — Oxford economist whose Doughnut Economics model has been adopted by Amsterdam, Copenhagen, and dozens of cities. Her TED talk has millions of views. Reimagines economics to meet human needs without overshooting planetary boundaries. Sophie Raworth — one of the BBC's most recognisable newsreaders.

What: Sister interviews sister about one of the most important economic ideas of the 21st century. The family dynamic adds warmth and honesty to what could be a dry policy conversation.
16:00
Climate Fiction Prize AI Pick
Stories that imagine our future
What: Climate fiction ("cli-fi") — one of the most vital literary genres of the decade. This session celebrates writing that imagines how we'll live with — and respond to — climate change. Samantha Harvey (Booker Prize winner for Orbital) is at the festival.

Why: A perfect way to close the festival — forward-looking, creative, hopeful. Take the ideas home with you.
🎲 Wildcards for Saturday
TBC · Emma Thompson — My Life in Books
National treasure. Oscar winner. Screenwriter. Activist. In conversation with Elizabeth Day. Funny, fierce, and probably beautifully sweary. Book now.
TBC · Dawn French — New Novel
The comedy legend discusses her latest fiction. Warm, hilarious, a perfect late-festival pick-me-up.
TBC · Nicholas Stern — Climate & Economics
Author of the Stern Review — the report that changed how governments think about climate costs. Pairs perfectly with the Climate Fiction Prize.
Evening · The Amy Winehouse Band
Amy's original band perform her music live. A dazzling, emotional performance to close out a brilliant week. The perfect last night.

Practical Notes

Base: Lower Court, Clifford — ~10 mins drive from the festival site
Between events: Allow 15 minutes between venues on site
Free site access: Dairy Meadows is free to enter — tickets only for individual events
Bookshops: Dozens of independents in Hay town — leave time to explore
Wild Garden: New for 2026 — a reflective space on site between sessions
MUBI Cinema: Free short film screenings — great for filling a gap
Hay Castle: Guided tours at 11am & 2pm daily. 800 years of history, Wye Valley views
Wildcard times: Marked TBC — check closer to the date and book early
Food & drink: Garden Kitchen on site — demos, tastings, great food
Focus themes: AI, politics, philosophy, nature
